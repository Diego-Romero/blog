Pomodoro as a Productivity Tool

By Diego Romero Reading Time 6 min Category Productivity
Formation / Contents
  • The Technique Explained
  • Why 25 Minutes Works
  • Adapting It for Deep Work
  • My Current Setup

The Technique Explained

The Pomodoro Technique was invented by Francesco Cirillo in the late 1980s. The name comes from a tomato-shaped kitchen timer he used as a university student. The method is deceptively minimal: work for 25 minutes, uninterrupted, then take a 5-minute break. Every four rounds, take a longer break of 15 to 30 minutes. That is the entire rulebook.

What strikes most people when they first try it is not the breaks — it is the enforced singularity of focus. You are not allowed to switch tasks mid-pomodoro. If a distraction surfaces, you write it down and return to it later. The pomodoro is treated as an atomic unit: indivisible.

One thing I did not expect was how the timer creates a kind of psychological safety. You are not working forever — you are working for 25 minutes. That is a manageable promise to make to yourself.

Diego Romero, in conversation with himself at 10pm

Why 25 Minutes Works

The 25-minute window is short enough to feel non-threatening, but long enough to get into a genuine flow state. Cognitive science research on attention suggests that sustained focus — real, high-quality focus — is difficult to maintain beyond 45 minutes without degradation. Twenty-five minutes sits comfortably inside that window.

The break is not just rest. It is cognitive consolidation. Memory research consistently shows that the mind continues to process and integrate information during rest periods. The 5-minute break is working even when you are not. This is the hidden advantage the technique's critics — who call it too simplistic — tend to miss.

There is also the momentum effect. Completing a pomodoro is a small win. Small wins compound. After a morning of six completed pomodoros, the sense of forward movement is tangible and motivating in a way that a vague "I worked hard today" never quite is.

Adapting It for Deep Work

Pure Pomodoro and Deep Work (Cal Newport's concept) exist in slight tension. Newport advocates for longer, sustained blocks — often 90 minutes or more. The 25-minute ceiling can feel restrictive if you are genuinely deep in a complex problem. My adaptation: I use the Pomodoro structure for planning and shallow work, and I allow myself to extend a session when I hit real flow. The timer becomes a floor, not a ceiling.

The key is using the technique's check-in discipline without becoming enslaved to its intervals. The moment the timer goes off is a prompt to ask: "Am I in flow, or am I coasting?" If flow, keep going. If coasting, the break is exactly what you need.

The Pomodoro timer is not a task manager. It is an attention manager. That reframe changes everything about how you use it.

A note in the margin of my copy of Deep Work

My Current Setup

I run Deepflow — my own focus timer app — which extends Pomodoro with session tagging and daily analytics. Each session gets a category label (coding, writing, reading, admin) and a brief note on what was accomplished. After a week, the data paints an accurate picture of where your attention actually goes, versus where you think it goes.

The gap between those two numbers is, consistently, humbling. Here is a minimal JavaScript timer to illustrate the core loop — the same logic that powers Deepflow: 

JavaScript
class PomodoroTimer {
  constructor(workMins = 25, breakMins = 5) {
    this.workMs   = workMins  * 60 * 1000;
    this.breakMs  = breakMins * 60 * 1000;
    this.round    = 0;
    this.interval = null;
  }

  start() {
    this.round++;
    const duration = this.workMs;
    const end      = Date.now() + duration;

    console.log(`Pomodoro ${this.round} — go.`);

    this.interval = setInterval(() => {
      const remaining = end - Date.now();

      if (remaining <= 0) {
        clearInterval(this.interval);
        this.onWorkComplete();
      }
    }, 1000);
  }

  onWorkComplete() {
    console.log(`Round ${this.round} done. Take a break.`);
    const isLongBreak = this.round % 4 === 0;
    const breakDur    = isLongBreak ? 20 * 60 * 1000 : this.breakMs;

    setTimeout(() => {
      console.log('Break over. Ready for the next round.');
      this.start();
    }, breakDur);
  }

  stop() {
    clearInterval(this.interval);
    console.log('Timer stopped.');
  }
}

const timer = new PomodoroTimer(25, 5);
timer.start();

The simplicity of the implementation is part of the point. The Pomodoro Technique is not a complex system — it is a simple contract with your own attention. The difficulty lies not in understanding it but in honouring it, session after session, day after day.

Start with one pomodoro tomorrow morning, before you check email, before you open Slack. See what 25 minutes of genuine focus produces. I suspect the answer will recalibrate your sense of what is possible in a working day.

Book Review · Pos 01

Shoe Dog

Phil Knight
Rating ★★★★★
Genre Memoir
Read Jan 2026
Pages 386

What It's About

Shoe Dog is Phil Knight's memoir of the years from 1962 to 1980, covering the improbable creation of Nike. It starts with Knight as a 24-year-old Stanford MBA graduate with a wild idea: import high-quality Japanese running shoes to the United States. It ends, roughly, when the company went public and he knew the thing would survive him. What happens in between is one of the most gripping business narratives ever put to paper.

Knight writes with unusual candour. He does not present himself as a visionary who always knew it would work. He presents himself as a man who was often terrified, frequently wrong, perpetually short of cash, and somehow unable to stop. That honesty is the book's greatest strength.

The Entrepreneurial Grit

What stayed with me most is the financial precariousness of Nike's early years. Knight operated with almost no working capital for most of the company's first decade. He would sell a batch of shoes, use the money to order more shoes before he had sold the first batch, and then scramble to find bridge financing to cover the gap. This cycle repeated — terrifyingly, relentlessly — year after year. The banks threatened to pull his credit lines. Japanese partners threatened to cut him off. Distributors stole his designs. He kept going.

The team he assembled is equally remarkable. Jeff Johnson, the first employee, would send Knight obsessive letters about shoe technology, store design, and marketing. Bob Woodell ran operations from a wheelchair after a swimming accident robbed him of the use of his legs. Bill Bowerman — Knight's old track coach — was gluing together shoe prototypes in his kitchen and eventually poured rubber into a waffle iron to create the iconic waffle sole. These are not corporate archetypes. They are oddballs who cared too much.

The cowards never started and the weak died along the way. That leaves us. Let me ask you, who would you rather be?

Phil Knight, Shoe Dog

What It Taught Me

I read this book while in the early stages of building Deepflow, and its timing was perfect. The specific lesson I took from it was about the relationship between conviction and information. Knight had very little evidence that Blue Ribbon Sports — as Nike was first called — would work. He had a hunch, some early sales numbers, and an unreasonable attachment to the idea. He moved anyway.

That is not a call to recklessness. Knight was methodical about what he could control: product quality, relationships with suppliers, team selection. He was comfortable with uncertainty about what he could not control: the market, competitor moves, macroeconomic conditions. Separating those two categories and acting decisively on the first is a skill I am still developing.

The book also reframed my understanding of what success means at the start of a venture. Nike "succeeded" multiple times before it actually succeeded — meaning it survived. Each crisis that Knight navigated was a version of the company dying and coming back. Durability is the underrated virtue.

I'd tell men and women in their mid-twenties not to settle for a job or a career or even a calling. I'd tell them to seek a cause. And if you're wondering what your cause is? I'd say: you'll know it when you find it. That's as specific as I can get.

Phil Knight, Shoe Dog

A Quote That Hit Hard

The quote above about "cause" is the one most readers highlight. But the line that stopped me cold was simpler, buried mid-narrative: "I wanted to win. Or maybe I just didn't want to lose. I'm still not sure there's a difference." That sentence carries the entire ambiguity of competitive drive. I have been thinking about it since.

If you build things, or want to, read this book. It will not give you a framework or a checklist. It will give you something harder to manufacture: the sense that someone else was exactly as scared as you are, and they kept going anyway.

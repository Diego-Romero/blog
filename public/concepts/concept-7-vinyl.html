On Deep Work and Focused Programming

Diego Romero January 2025 8 min read

There is a moment every programmer knows: the zone. Everything else dissolves. The problem becomes the only thing. An hour passes, maybe two. Then your phone buzzes, or Slack pings, or a calendar reminder fires — and the thread is broken. You sit staring at the screen wondering where you were, what you were thinking, how you had held all those moving pieces in your head at once. It takes another fifteen minutes to get back. But often you never quite do.

The Myth of Multitasking

We have sold ourselves a story about the modern knowledge worker: efficient, parallel, always-on. The story is false. Neuroscience has consistently shown that what we call multitasking is simply rapid context-switching — and every switch carries a cost. Gloria Mark at UC Irvine found it takes an average of 23 minutes to fully return to a task after being interrupted. Twenty-three minutes. If you field six interruptions in a workday, you have potentially lost more than two hours to re-entry overhead alone — before accounting for the quality degradation of the work itself.

Software engineering is particularly vulnerable to this dynamic. Writing code is not like writing email. It requires holding a mental model of a system — data flowing through layers, state transforming across components, edge cases lurking in business logic — and that model collapses the moment attention is diverted. The cost is not just time. It is also correctness. Bugs cluster in code written in distracted states.

Efforts to deepen your focus will struggle if you don't simultaneously wean yourself from a dependence on distraction.

— Cal Newport, Deep Work

Newport's Framework

Cal Newport's core argument in Deep Work is deceptively simple: the ability to focus without distraction on a cognitively demanding task is becoming simultaneously rarer and more valuable. The people who cultivate this ability will thrive; those who don't will fall behind. Newport is not moralising about laziness. He is describing a structural shift in what creates economic value in an age where shallow tasks can increasingly be automated or outsourced.

His framework has two main pillars. First, you must schedule deep work like a professional commitment — not something you do when you happen to find spare time, because spare time never comes. Second, you must embrace boredom. Newport argues that the capacity to resist distraction is a trained skill, and you train it by tolerating the discomfort of not reaching for your phone every idle moment. This was the insight that struck me hardest. I had treated distraction as a failure of willpower. Newport reframes it as a failure of practice.

He also distinguishes between different scheduling philosophies: the monastic approach (eliminate almost all shallow obligations permanently), the bimodal approach (alternate between deep and shallow seasons), the rhythmic approach (make a daily ritual of deep work), and the journalistic approach (seize deep work windows wherever they appear). For most software engineers working in organisations, the rhythmic approach is the most realistic.

A deep life is a good life, any way you look at it.

— Cal Newport, Deep Work

What I Changed

I made three concrete changes. First, I blocked 7–10am as a protected coding window. No meetings, no Slack, no email. The calendar entry is called "Studio Time" and I treat it as sacred as a client call. Second, I turned off all notification badges on my laptop — permanently, not just during focus sessions. The ambient awareness that notifications might exist was enough to pull me toward checking. Removing the badges removed the pull.

Third — and this is the one that made the biggest difference — I started using a Pomodoro-style timer, but with one modification: I extended the work interval to 52 minutes and the break to 17 minutes, based on research from the Draugiem Group suggesting this ratio corresponds to the highest-productivity patterns among knowledge workers. I built a small timer into my workflow and stopped second-guessing whether I was being productive enough. The timer became the arbiter. When it ran, I worked. When it stopped, I rested. The decision-making overhead vanished.

The Stack That Helps

I will not pretend tools matter as much as habits. They don't. But the right tools reduce friction, and reduced friction compounds over time. Here is what I actually use: a 13-inch laptop with an external monitor for the secondary browser, a mechanical keyboard (the tactile feedback helps me stay anchored in the physical present), and a set of noise-cancelling headphones playing brown noise — not music, which competes with the verbal parts of thinking.

On the software side, I wrote a small Pomodoro timer in TypeScript that lives in my menu bar. It does nothing except count down and log session data to a local JSON file. Over weeks, that data told me which hours I completed sessions most consistently, which types of tasks broke my focus fastest, and which days of the week I was most likely to skip deep work entirely. Data, as it turns out, is motivating.

TypeScriptpomodoro.ts
type Session = {
  id: string;
  startedAt: Date;
  duration: number; // minutes
  completed: boolean;
  label?: string;
};

function createPomodoro(durationMinutes = 52) {
  let remaining = durationMinutes * 60; // seconds
  let intervalId: ReturnType<typeof setInterval> | null = null;
  const session: Session = {
    id: crypto.randomUUID(),
    startedAt: new Date(),
    duration: durationMinutes,
    completed: false,
  };

  function start(label?: string): void {
    session.label = label;
    intervalId = setInterval(() => {
      remaining -= 1;
      if (remaining <= 0) {
        complete();
      }
    }, 1000);
  }

  function complete(): void {
    if (intervalId) clearInterval(intervalId);
    session.completed = true;
    logSession(session);
    notifyUser("Session complete. Take your break.");
  }

  function pause(): void {
    if (intervalId) clearInterval(intervalId);
    intervalId = null;
  }

  function getTimeRemaining(): string {
    const m = Math.floor(remaining / 60).toString().padStart(2, "0");
    const s = (remaining % 60).toString().padStart(2, "0");
    return `${m}:${s}`;
  }

  return { start, pause, getTimeRemaining };
}

function notifyUser(message: string): void {
  new Notification("Deepflow", { body: message });
}

function logSession(session: Session): void {
  // Append to local JSON log for tracking streaks
  console.log("[session]", JSON.stringify(session, null, 2));
}

// Usage
const timer = createPomodoro(52);
timer.start("Refactor auth module");
console.log(timer.getTimeRemaining()); // "52:00"

The code above is the core of Deepflow, the focus timer I eventually open-sourced. It is deliberately simple. It does not integrate with your calendar, it does not surface analytics dashboards, it does not have a social layer. It counts down. It logs. That is the entire surface area. Complexity is the enemy of consistency, and consistency is the only thing that matters.

Deep work is not a productivity hack. It is a reorientation toward what you actually value. When you build the habit, you discover not only that you produce more — but that the work feels more like work worth doing. That feedback loop is self-sustaining in a way that no notification blocker, no app, no morning routine ever is on its own.

What The Book Is About

The Psychology of Money is not a book about how to pick stocks or optimise a portfolio. Morgan Housel — a partner at the Collaborative Fund and former columnist at the Wall Street Journal — is interested in something more fundamental: why smart, educated people make terrible financial decisions, and why ordinary people with no finance background sometimes accumulate remarkable wealth. His answer, developed across 19 interconnected essays, is that financial outcomes are determined less by spreadsheet intelligence and more by behaviour. Specifically, by the stories we tell ourselves about money, risk, time, and luck.

The book arrived in 2020, in the middle of a pandemic that scrambled every economic assumption, and it felt immediately essential. It was not giving advice calibrated to a bull market or a particular macroeconomic regime. It was talking about human nature — which is the one constant the market always prices in last.

The Insight That Stuck

Of all Housel's ideas, the one I return to most is his distinction between being wealthy and being rich. Being rich means having a high income or visible assets — the car, the watch, the house in the right postcode. Being wealthy means having unspent financial assets: options, time, choices. Wealth is invisible by definition. We cannot see it. And because we cannot see it, we systematically undervalue it and overvalue the performance of richness.

Wealth is what you don't see. It's the cars not purchased, the diamonds not bought, the renovations postponed, the clothes forgone and the first-class upgrade declined. It's assets in the bank that haven't yet been converted into the stuff you see.

— Morgan Housel, The Psychology of Money

This hit me harder than I expected. I had spent years thinking about financial independence in terms of income milestones — if I earn this much, then I will be fine. Housel reframes the equation entirely: it is not about what you earn but about the gap between what you earn and what you spend, maintained consistently over time. Compounding does the rest.

Key Ideas

  • Luck and risk are siblings. Every outcome has a component that was never in your control. Acknowledging this makes you more generous toward others' failures and less arrogant about your own successes.
  • Enough is underrated. The inability to stop goal-posts moving is one of the most dangerous financial traits. Knowing when you have enough requires genuine self-knowledge, not just a higher number.
  • Tail events drive everything. A small number of outcomes account for the majority of results in investing, careers, and business. Getting comfortable with long periods of mediocrity in order to be present for the rare exceptional moment is a skill.
  • You are not a spreadsheet. Saving money in a way that causes you daily anxiety is not a rational strategy even if the numbers say it is. The best financial plan is the one you can stick to.
  • The price of admission. Volatility is not a bug in markets — it is the fee for long-term returns. Reframing it as a cost rather than a punishment changes how you endure downturns.

The highest form of wealth is the ability to wake up every morning and say, "I can do whatever I want today."

— Morgan Housel, The Psychology of Money

Who Should Read This

Everyone, without qualification — but especially software engineers and others in high-income, high-volatility careers who are prone to lifestyle inflation and scope-creep in ambition. The book is short, deliberately readable, and does not require any prior knowledge of finance. Housel writes in plain English about complex human dynamics, and that combination is genuinely rare. I have recommended it to a family member who has never invested a penny and to a colleague who manages a venture fund. Both found it valuable in completely different ways. That is the mark of a book that has genuinely found something true.

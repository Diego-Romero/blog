Rewriting My Blog
With Claude Code

What happens when you hand an AI agent the keys to your codebase.

tech ai next.js

The Old Stack

My blog has lived in a few places over the years. First a static HTML file, then a WordPress install I immediately regretted, then a Gatsby site that took four hours to configure. The last version was a Next.js 13 project that worked perfectly well — except I never shipped anything because I was always tinkering with it instead of writing.

The codebase was a graveyard of half-implemented features: a dark mode toggle that toggled nothing, a tag system with one tag, a reading progress bar that measured the wrong element. Every time I sat down to write a post, I ended up refactoring the PostCard component instead. Classic programmer trap.

The real problem wasn't the stack — it was that I was using code complexity as a form of procrastination. The blog became the thing I worked on instead of the thing I published to.

Enter Claude Code

I'd been using Claude for code review and rubber-ducking for months. Then Anthropic released Claude Code — an agent that lives in your terminal and can read, write, and modify files directly. My first reaction was "this will hallucinate my CSS into oblivion." My second reaction was to try it anyway.

The best way to understand a new tool is to use it on something you care about. I cared about getting this blog out of my local environment and onto the internet.

My internal monologue, February 2026

The workflow is simple: you open a terminal session, point Claude Code at your project, and describe what you want. It reads your files, proposes changes, and executes them after you approve. The key insight is that it maintains context across a session — it remembers that you told it to use TypeScript strict mode three prompts ago.

I started with a prompt like: "This is a Next.js blog. I want a clean design with dark mode, a reading list, and a projects section. Use Tailwind. Don't touch the MDX setup." Within about forty minutes of back-and-forth, I had something that looked better than what I'd built in six months of weekend sessions.

What I Built

The new blog is still Next.js — App Router this time. The content is MDX files with a clean frontmatter schema. Here's the type definition I landed on after a few iterations with the agent:

TypeScript
interface BlogPost {
  slug: string;
  title: string;
  date: string;
  excerpt: string;
  tags: string[];
  readingTime: number; // minutes
  published: boolean;
  coverImage?: string;
  bookReview?: {
    author: string;
    rating: 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5;
    isbn?: string;
  };
}

export async function getAllPosts(): Promise<BlogPost[]> {
  const files = await fs.readdir('content/posts');
  return files
    .filter(f => f.endsWith('.mdx'))
    .map(parsePost)
    .filter(p => p.published);
}

The bookReview optional field was Claude's suggestion. I'd mentioned I wanted to post book reviews alongside regular articles, and it quietly extended the type to support that without me explicitly asking. Small thing, but it showed the agent was reasoning about the larger picture.

What Changed In How I Work

The biggest shift wasn't technical — it was psychological. When I know I can describe a feature in plain English and have a working implementation in minutes, the activation energy for shipping drops dramatically. The blog went from something I maintained to something I wrote on.

Software authorship is changing. The question is no longer "can I build this?" — it's "do I understand what I built well enough to own it?"

A thought I had at 11pm on a Tuesday

I still review every change. I still understand the codebase. But I've stopped treating the implementation as the interesting part. The interesting part is what I'm trying to say — and now the code is just the medium for saying it.

Claude Code didn't write this post. But it did get out of the way so I could.

The Courage to Be Disliked

Ichiro Kishimi & Fumitake Koga

★★★★★  —  essential reading
📄 contents
├── The Socratic Structure ├── Adlerian Psychology Explained ├── The Insight About Separation of Tasks └── Who This Book Is For

The Socratic Structure

The book arrives as a five-night dialogue between a young man and a philosopher. The young man is angry, certain the world is unjust, and convinced that his past has determined his present. The philosopher listens, nods, and dismantles every argument so gently that you almost miss how radical the conclusions are.

This structure is not a gimmick. Kishimi and Koga chose the Socratic dialogue form deliberately — because the ideas they're presenting resist passive consumption. You need to argue against them. The young man in the book does that for you, and his objections are usually exactly what you're thinking. It reads less like a self-help book and more like sitting in on a philosophy seminar where the professor is infuriatingly calm.

The pacing is slow by design. Each of the five nights introduces a single, disorienting idea, and the nights that follow circle back to test whether you've really accepted it. By night three, I had underlined more passages than I had in any book in years.

Adlerian Psychology Explained

Alfred Adler was Freud's contemporary and eventual critic. Where Freud believed the past causes the present — trauma shapes behavior — Adler argued the opposite: we use the past to serve present goals. We don't have a trauma-caused fear of social situations; we cultivate anxiety because it excuses us from risking failure.

This is, to put it mildly, an uncomfortable idea. The book doesn't soften it. The philosopher tells the young man, with genuine warmth, that his unhappiness is a choice — not because he's broken, but because it's currently the safest option available to him.

No matter what has occurred in your life up to this point, it should have no bearing at all on how you live from now on. You are not determined by your past — you determine your life in this moment.

The Philosopher, Night One

I initially resisted this framing because it felt like it was dismissing real suffering. But Adler — as channeled by Kishimi and Koga — is not saying pain isn't real. He's saying that the story we build around pain is a construction, and constructions can be rebuilt. That distinction took me a few chapters to absorb.

The Insight About Separation of Tasks

The most practically useful concept in the book is what the authors call the "separation of tasks." The core idea: every problem in interpersonal relationships comes from intruding on someone else's tasks, or letting them intrude on yours.

Your task is how you behave. Someone else's task is how they respond to your behavior. You are responsible for the first; you have no business controlling the second. If you're kind to a colleague and they respond coldly, their coldness is their task — not yours to fix, not yours to be hurt by.

Applied consistently, this is liberating in a way that's hard to describe without making it sound trivial. The book isn't saying "don't care about others." It's saying that care expressed through control is not really care. Letting people own their own tasks is a form of respect.

All you can do with regard to your own life is choose the best path that you believe in. On the other hand, what judgment other people pass on that choice — that is the task of other people, and is not something you have any control over.

The Philosopher, Night Three

Who This Book Is For

Honestly? Everyone. But especially anyone who has felt stuck in a story about themselves — the one where your childhood, your circumstances, your personality type explain why things aren't going the way you want. This book doesn't tell you those stories are wrong. It asks whether they're useful.

It's also an unusually good book for engineers and technical people, who tend to be precise about external systems but surprisingly imprecise about their own psychology. The clarity of the Adlerian framework — all behavior is purposive, all unhappiness is relational, freedom is achieved through contribution — maps well onto a systems-thinking mindset.

Read it slowly. Argue with it. By the time the young man concedes his final point on night five, you'll probably have conceded a few things yourself.

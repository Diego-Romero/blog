Rewriting My Blog With Claude Code

Feb 10, 2026 8 min read AI · Dev

The Old Blog Was Holding Me Back

The previous iteration of this blog had been running on a Jekyll setup I cobbled together in 2019. At the time, it was exactly what I needed — static, fast, zero-cost to host. But over the years it had accumulated layers of workarounds, outdated dependencies, and a content model that made adding new post types feel like archaeology. Every time I wanted to publish something, I was fighting the tooling as much as I was writing.

The deeper problem was not technical debt in the traditional sense. It was that the blog no longer reflected how I thought about my work. I wanted a place that could hold book notes alongside technical writing, project case studies alongside personal reflections. The old structure was a single timeline of markdown files. Adequate, but not expressive.

I had been putting off the rewrite for over a year — not because I did not know what I wanted, but because the cost of doing it alone, across late evenings and weekends, felt too high relative to the benefit. That changed when I started experimenting seriously with Claude Code.

Choosing Claude Code as My Collaborator

I had used language models for code generation before — primarily in the form of autocomplete and isolated snippets. What made Claude Code different was the sense that I was working with something that could hold context across the whole project. Not just a function, but a system. I could describe the content model I wanted, the design direction I was moving toward, and the technical constraints I cared about — and get back something coherent enough to build on.

The workflow that emerged was iterative and conversational. I would describe what I was trying to accomplish in plain terms, review what came back, ask for adjustments, and occasionally override decisions I disagreed with. The AI wrote more of the boilerplate than I did. I wrote more of the architecture decisions and content than it did. The division of labor felt natural after a day or two.

What I did not expect was how useful Claude Code would be as a thinking partner during the early design phase. Asking it to articulate the tradeoffs between two approaches — even when I already had a preference — often surfaced considerations I had not consciously named. It has a useful bias toward exhaustiveness that I sometimes lack.

The Architecture I Landed On

The content layer is TypeScript-first, with explicit interfaces for each content type. A post is not just markdown with frontmatter — it has a known shape the rest of the application can depend on. This makes the rendering layer significantly simpler. Here is a simplified version of the core interfaces: 

interface Post {
  slug: string;
  title: string;
  date: string;
  excerpt: string;
  readingTime: number;
  tags: string[];
  body: string;
}

interface BookNote {
  slug: string;
  title: string;
  author: string;
  rating: 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5;
  genre: string;
  pages: number;
  readDate: string;
  coverColor: string;
  body: string;
  keyTakeaways: string[];
}

interface Project {
  slug: string;
  name: string;
  type: string;
  description: string;
  techStack: string[];
  links: { label: string; url: string }[];
}

Having these types defined early made subsequent decisions easier. The rendering components are pure functions from data to HTML. There is no magic, no implicit structure — just typed data flowing through typed functions. It is the kind of architecture that is boring to describe and pleasant to maintain.

What I Learned

The most valuable thing about working with an AI collaborator is not the code it produces. It is the clarity you gain from having to articulate what you actually want. — From my notes, January 2026

Several things became clear over the course of this rewrite:

  • Writing a detailed brief before generating anything produces dramatically better results than iterating from a blank prompt.
  • The AI is a strong executor and a useful sounding board, but architectural taste still has to come from the human.
  • Reviewing generated code carefully — and understanding it fully — is not optional. You own whatever you ship.
  • The time saved on boilerplate is best reinvested in writing better content, not generating more code.
  • Constraints are generative. The more specific and opinionated the brief, the more useful the output.

What This Book Is About

Deep Work is Cal Newport's argument that the ability to focus without distraction on cognitively demanding tasks — what he calls "deep work" — is both increasingly rare and increasingly valuable in our economy. The premise is straightforward, almost obvious when stated plainly. But Newport builds the case with unusual rigour, weaving together neuroscience, economics, and philosophy to explain why our current culture of connectivity is actively hostile to the kind of thinking that produces meaningful work.

Newport distinguishes between "deep work" — focused, distraction-free cognitive effort — and "shallow work" — logistical tasks that are often performed while distracted and do not create much new value. He argues that knowledge workers have drifted toward shallow work not because it is more valuable, but because it is more visible, more comfortable, and more socially rewarded in organisations that mistake busyness for productivity.

The second half of the book is practical: a set of strategies and rules for building a life organized around depth. Newport is prescriptive in a way that some readers find too rigid, but I found the specificity refreshing. Vague advice about "focusing more" is abundant. A concrete system is rare.

Why It Resonated

I read this book at a point when I had been feeling, without quite being able to articulate it, that my best work was happening in shorter and shorter windows. The mornings, before meetings began and Slack woke up, were the only hours where I felt genuinely productive. The rest of the day was responsive, reactive, often useful — but not generative in the same way. Newport gave me language for what I was experiencing and a framework for defending those morning hours more deliberately.

The most immediately useful shift was reducing the frequency with which I checked communication channels. Newport's argument — that the cognitive cost of task-switching is higher than it appears, and that the recovery time after distraction is longer than people assume — was not entirely new to me. But seeing it articulated clearly, alongside specific interventions, made it actionable in a way that vague awareness had not.

I began scheduling "deep work blocks" in my calendar, treating them as non-negotiable as external meetings. Within two weeks the quality of what I was producing in those blocks — code, writing, design thinking — had measurably improved. Not because I had become smarter, but because I was giving difficult problems sustained attention they had not been receiving before.

Key Takeaways

  • Deep work — focused, undistracted effort — is the key differentiator in a knowledge economy increasingly dominated by shallow, reactive work.
  • The ability to concentrate deeply is a skill that atrophies without deliberate practice and active protection.
  • Context-switching has a much higher cognitive cost than it appears; each interruption takes significant time to recover from.
  • Scheduling deep work blocks in advance, and treating them as non-negotiable, is more effective than trying to "find time" reactively.
  • Embracing boredom — resisting the urge to reach for distraction during downtime — trains the concentration muscle outside of work hours.
  • Draining shallow obligations and protecting time for depth is a professional and ethical stance, not merely a productivity tactic.
"The ability to perform deep work is becoming increasingly rare at exactly the same time it is becoming increasingly valuable in our economy." — Cal Newport, Deep Work
"Human beings, it seems, are at their best when immersed deeply in something challenging." — Cal Newport, Deep Work

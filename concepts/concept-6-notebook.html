The Old Blog Claude Code Architecture What I Learned
Feb. 10th, 2026

Rewriting My Blog With Claude Code

ai dev 6 min read

The Old Blog Was Holding Me Back

I started this blog three years ago on a framework that felt cutting-edge at the time. I won't name it — there's enough tribal fighting in the frontend world without me adding to it. What I will say is that by early 2025, every time I wanted to publish something, I found myself fighting the tooling instead of writing.

The build pipeline had seventeen steps. The config files had config files. I had a tailwind.config.js that referenced a theme.config.js that imported values from a design-tokens.json that I hadn't touched in two years. It was archaeology every time I needed to change a color.

"The irony is that I had built the most flexible possible system — and the flexibility had become the cage. When everything is configurable, nothing is decided."

I needed a fresh start. And I wanted to try something different: instead of architecting it myself from scratch, I wanted to see what would happen if I described what I wanted to a capable AI and let it do the heavy lifting.

Choosing Claude Code as My Collaborator

I had been using AI assistants for code since the GPT-3 era, but Claude Code felt different. The context window was large enough to hold an entire codebase in mind. The tool-calling let it actually run commands, read files, and see the results. It wasn't autocomplete — it was something closer to pair programming.

My workflow was simple: I described the blog I wanted in plain English. What it should look like. How it should feel. What content I had. Then I let Claude Code work, watching the files materialize and asking questions when something surprised me.

The first surprise was how much better its first draft was than mine would have been. Not because it knew more about design — it didn't — but because it didn't carry the mental baggage I did. It hadn't spent three years accumulating small compromises. It just built the thing I described.

The Architecture I Landed On

After a few iterations, we landed on Next.js with a content layer that reads from MDX files. Nothing revolutionary. But the structure was clean in a way my previous site never was: 

/app
  /blog
    /[slug]
      page.tsx
  /books
    page.tsx
  /projects
    page.tsx
/content
  /posts
    *.mdx
  /books
    *.mdx
/components
  Article.tsx
  BookCard.tsx
  TagStamp.tsx
/lib
  content.ts   # single source of truth for data
  utils.ts

The key insight — which Claude Code kept nudging me toward — was that the content schema should drive the component design, not the other way around. When you start with a clean data model, the UI practically writes itself.

I also wrote almost none of the CSS myself. I described the visual direction ("ruled notebook, analog feel, warm paper tones") and reviewed what came back. Sometimes I pushed back. Mostly I accepted it and moved on.

What I Learned

The thing that struck me most was how quickly my role shifted. I went from being the person who writes code to being the person who decides what the code should do and whether it did it well. The judgment part — the taste — that's still entirely mine. The implementation became almost incidental.

I don't think this is loss. I think it's a transfer of effort toward the parts that actually matter: clarity of thought, editorial judgment, knowing what you want and why. The tools will keep improving. The harder skill — having something worth saying and knowing how to say it — that's what I want to spend my time on.

"Build faster so you can think more. The goal was never the scaffolding — it was always what you'd put inside it."

The blog you're reading right now is the result. It took a weekend instead of a month. I'm not sure I could have done it better by hand — and I'm certain I wouldn't have had as much fun doing it.

Must Read

Deep Work

Summary:

Newport's core claim is deceptively simple: in an increasingly distracted world, the ability to focus without distraction on cognitively demanding work is both rare and extremely valuable. He calls this "deep work" — and he argues that most knowledge workers are systematically destroying their ability to do it.

The book is split into two parts. The first makes the case that deep work is valuable and becoming rarer. The second is a practical manual for how to cultivate it: scheduling strategies, attention training, productivity philosophies. It is unusually concrete for a business book.

"The ability to perform deep work is becoming increasingly rare at exactly the same time it is becoming increasingly valuable in our economy. The few who cultivate this skill, and then make it the core of their working life, will thrive."
— Cal Newport, Deep Work

My Thoughts:

I came to this book a skeptic. The premise sounded like productivity-book marketing copy. By page forty I was taking notes in the margins. Newport is the rare business author who backs his arguments with evidence, cites his sources, and doesn't repeat himself.

What I found most compelling was the chapter on attention residue — the phenomenon where switching between tasks leaves a "residue" of attention on the previous task, degrading your capacity for the next one. This explains why my mornings feel different when I check email first versus when I don't. It wasn't discipline I was missing; it was understanding of how attention actually works.

"Clarity about what matters provides clarity about what does not."
— my favorite line in the book

Things I Do Differently Now:

  1. Email is checked twice a day — 10am and 4pm. Not before, not between.
  2. My calendar has 4-hour "deep blocks" that are treated like meetings with my most important client.
  3. Slack is closed during deep blocks. The world has not ended.
  4. I track my deep work hours each week. Aim for 20. It's harder than it sounds.
  5. I've stopped multitasking as a default. I finish one thing before starting the next.

If you work with information and ideas for a living, read this book. It will not tell you anything comfortable, but it will tell you things that are true.

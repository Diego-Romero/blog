Rewriting My Blog With Claude Code

The Old Blog Was Holding Me Back

The previous version of this blog was built with Next.js 14, Tailwind CSS, MDX, a custom content layer, and about a dozen npm packages whose sole purpose was to make other packages work. It was, objectively, too much. Every time I wanted to write something, I'd open the project and spend fifteen minutes fighting the toolchain instead of actually writing.

The irony wasn't lost on me: I was a software engineer who had engineered himself into a situation where publishing a post felt like deploying a microservice. Syntax highlighting config, frontmatter schemas, TypeScript type errors from packages that hadn't been updated in two years — it was friction everywhere. The kind of friction that, over weeks, becomes a reason not to write at all.

I wanted something leaner. Something where the act of writing and the act of publishing were as close together as possible. What I didn't anticipate was that rebuilding it would teach me as much about how I work as it did about how software works.

Choosing Claude Code as My Collaborator

I'd been using Claude in the browser for months — mostly for code review, rubber-duck debugging, and talking through architecture decisions. The Claude Code CLI felt like a natural extension of that workflow, except now the model could actually see my project, run commands, read files, and iterate in the context of real code rather than pasted snippets.

The workflow I landed on was almost conversational. I'd describe what I wanted — "a blog post type with slug, title, date, tags, reading time, and markdown content" — and Claude Code would propose the structure, write the types, scaffold the component, and point out edge cases I hadn't considered. I wasn't just prompting; I was pair programming. The difference matters. With pair programming, you're both accountable to the code.

What surprised me most was how much faster I moved on decisions I'd normally agonize over. When a collaborator proposes something reasonable and explains their reasoning, "good enough, ship it" becomes the right call far more often than "let me spend two hours researching the optimal approach." Speed comes from reduced decision friction, not from typing faster.

The Architecture I Landed On

The final structure is deliberately simple. Blog posts are Markdown files in a /content directory. A small TypeScript module reads, parses, and sorts them at build time. No database, no CMS, no external API calls. The content type ended up looking like this: 

// types/blog.ts

interface BlogPost {
  slug:        string;
  title:       string;
  date:        string;
  readingTime: number;
  tags:        string[];
  content:     string;
  excerpt?:    string;
  published:   boolean;
}

interface Book {
  slug:    string;
  title:   string;
  author:  string;
  rating:  number;
  genre:   string;
  review:  string;
  readAt:  string;
  pages:   number;
  cover:   string;
}

export type { BlogPost, Book };

That's it. No remark plugin pipeline, no complex schema validation, no generated types. Just a plain interface that describes what a post is. The rendering is handled by a single marked call with a syntax highlighting plugin. The whole content module is under 80 lines.

What I Learned About Writing and Building

"The best tool is the one that disappears. You stop thinking about it and start thinking about what you're actually trying to say."

— Something I wrote in my notes around 2 AM during the rewrite

There's a version of this post where I evangelize AI-assisted development as a category-defining shift. I'm going to resist that framing, because the truth is more specific and more useful: Claude Code was useful to me, in this project, because it reduced the cost of exploring an idea. Not every idea — but enough of them that the work moved faster and I stayed curious instead of getting bogged down.

Here's what actually changed in how I work:

  • I write the intention first, in plain English, before writing any code. It forces clarity I used to skip over.
  • Overengineering is now immediately visible. When I describe something convoluted, the response reflects that convolusion back at me.
  • Decision debt accumulates slower. When something is proposed and I accept it, I've thought about it at least once. That's better than my usual process of copy-pasting from Stack Overflow and moving on.
  • The gap between "I want to try this" and "I have a working prototype" is much shorter — which means more ideas get tested instead of remaining hypothetical.
  • Code I didn't write is still code I'm responsible for. That discipline matters. Reading everything Claude Code produced was non-negotiable.

The blog you're reading right now is the outcome. It loads fast, it's easy to add to, and I actually want to open it and write things. That's the whole goal.

DEEP WORK
by Cal Newport

What This Book Is About

Deep Work is Cal Newport's argument that the ability to focus without distraction on cognitively demanding tasks is both increasingly rare and increasingly valuable in our modern economy. Newport defines deep work as "professional activities performed in a state of distraction-free concentration that push your cognitive capabilities to their limit." The counterpoint — shallow work — is logistical, replicable, and does not create much new value in the world.

The book is structured in two parts. The first makes the case for why deep work matters: drawing on examples from Carl Jung, J.K. Rowling, and various academics and entrepreneurs, Newport argues that the people who produce the most meaningful output in their fields are those who have protected their attention most ferociously. The second part is practical — four rules for incorporating deep work into a normal professional life.

Newport doesn't moralize or claim that shallow work is inherently bad. His position is simpler and more brutal: if you're trying to produce things that are genuinely hard and genuinely valuable, shallow work will crowd out the conditions under which those things are possible. The math is unforgiving. Attention is finite. How you spend it determines what you can produce.

Why It Resonated

I read Deep Work at a moment when I'd started noticing a pattern in my own work: I was perpetually busy, perpetually behind, and producing less than I felt capable of. My calendar was full of meetings. My Slack was always open. I was reachable by default and unavailable for actual thinking by accident. I recognized myself immediately in Newport's description of the shallow worker — not because I was lazy, but because the environment I'd built for myself made depth structurally impossible.

What hit hardest was Newport's observation that knowledge workers often can't tell the difference between being productive and appearing productive. The same behaviors that signal effort in an office — rapid email responses, always available on chat, in every meeting — are often the exact behaviors that prevent the kind of focused work that actually moves things forward. I had been optimizing for looking productive rather than being effective.

After finishing the book I made several changes that have stuck. I block 3-hour deep work sessions in my calendar three times a week. My phone stays face-down and silent during those blocks. I check email twice a day instead of reactively. These changes are small in absolute terms, but the compounding effect on output has been substantial. I produce more, and what I produce is better.

Key Takeaways

  • Deep work is a skill that atrophies without deliberate practice — you cannot context-switch all day and then flip a switch into focus mode at will.
  • Schedule every minute of your workday (loosely). Not to be rigid, but to force intentionality about how you're spending time.
  • Embrace boredom. The urge to reach for your phone in every idle moment trains your brain to expect constant stimulation — which makes sustained focus harder.
  • The "any benefit" justification for social media and distraction tools is insufficient. Evaluate tools by their actual contribution to your goals, not just their absence of obvious harm.
  • Shutdown rituals matter. A clear end to the workday signals to your brain that incomplete tasks are captured and can be released from active processing — which protects recovery time.
  • Most knowledge workers never push their cognitive limits. The ceiling is much higher than daily shallow work would suggest, but you can't find it without extended, uninterrupted effort.

"If you don't produce, you won't thrive — no matter how skilled or talented you are."

— Cal Newport, Deep Work

"The ability to perform deep work is becoming increasingly rare at exactly the same time it is becoming increasingly valuable in our economy. The few who cultivate this skill, and then make it the core of their working life, will thrive."

— Cal Newport, Deep Work

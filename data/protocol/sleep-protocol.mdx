Sleep is the foundation. Training, nutrition, focus, mood — everything degrades when sleep quality drops. This protocol covers the environment, timing, tracking, and troubleshooting that make 7-8 hours of quality sleep the default, not the exception.

**Target:** 7-7.5 hours of sleep per night
**Anchor:** Fixed wake time — same time every day, no exceptions
**Principle:** Protect the environment, fix the schedule, track the metrics

---

## The Two Rules

### Rule 1: Fixed Wake Time

Wake up at the same time every single day — weekdays, weekends, holidays, vacations. Your circadian rhythm doesn't know what day it is. Sleeping in on weekends gives you social jet lag every Monday.

If you went to bed late, still wake up at your normal time. You'll be tired, and that's the corrective signal — you'll go to bed earlier that night, self-correcting without shifting your entire schedule. The moment you start negotiating your wake time, the whole system drifts.

### Rule 2: Fixed Wind-Down

Start your evening protocol at the same time every night (see Evening Protocol). The wind-down ritual is the signal that tells your brain it's time to transition to sleep. Consistency matters more than duration — a 30-minute compressed wind-down at the same time beats a perfect 60-minute routine that starts at a different hour every night.

## Sleep Environment

### Temperature

This is the highest-leverage change you can make. Your core body temperature needs to drop ~1-2°F to initiate sleep. A warm room fights this process.

- **Room temperature:** 65-68°F (18-20°C) — cooler than feels natural when you're awake
- **Temperature-controlled mattress** (if available): Set to cool during the first half of the night, slightly warmer toward morning. This mimics your body's natural temperature cycle.
- **Hot shower before bed → rapid cool-down:** A warm shower 30-60 minutes before bed raises your skin temperature, which accelerates core temperature drop afterward. Counter-intuitive but effective.

### Light

Your circadian rhythm is primarily driven by light exposure. Getting this wrong is the most common cause of poor sleep onset.

- **Morning:** Get bright light (ideally sunlight) within 30 minutes of waking. This sets your circadian clock and triggers a cortisol pulse that makes you alert. If you commute to the gym on foot, you get this for free.
- **Evening:** Dim all lights to warm/amber after sunset. Overhead fluorescent or cool-white LEDs suppress melatonin production. Use warm lamps, candles, or smart bulbs set to 2700K or lower.
- **Bedroom:** Blackout curtains or a sleep mask. Any ambient light — streetlights, standby LEDs, phone screens — disrupts deep sleep even if you don't consciously notice it.
- **No screens in the last 30 minutes.** If you must use a device, use dark mode + night shift / blue light filter at maximum warmth. But a physical book is better.

### Sound

- **Consistent background:** White noise machine, fan, or consistent HVAC. The problem isn't noise — it's *variable* noise (sirens, neighbors, random sounds). A constant background masks the variability.
- **Earplugs:** Keep a pair on the nightstand for noisy nights.

### Phone

The phone is the single biggest sleep disruptor, and it's not the blue light — it's the stimulation. One notification or one interesting thread and your brain is fully awake.

- Phone charges **outside the bedroom** or face-down on Do Not Disturb
- Use a dedicated alarm clock instead of your phone (or a smart mattress alarm)
- If you use your phone as an alarm, put it across the room so you have to get up to turn it off

## Timing

### Bedtime Calculation

Work backward from your fixed wake time:

| Variable | Example |
|----------|---------|
| **Wake time** | 6:00 AM |
| **Sleep target** | 7.5 hours |
| **Sleep onset** (~15 min to fall asleep) | 10:15 PM |
| **Start wind-down** (60 min before sleep onset) | 9:15 PM |
| **In bed by** | 10:00-10:15 PM |

Adjust for your own wake time and sleep needs. Most adults need 7-9 hours. Track for a few weeks to find your personal optimum — the amount where you wake up feeling rested without an alarm.

### Late Night Adjustments

Social events, activities, or travel will occasionally push bedtime later. The rules:

- **Wake time never moves.** This is non-negotiable. A tired day is temporary; a shifted schedule takes days to recover from.
- **Use the compressed wind-down** (30 min) when you get home late. Some deceleration is better than none.
- **Accept the shorter night.** You'll sleep slightly less, be slightly tired the next day, and naturally fall asleep earlier the following night. The system self-corrects if you keep the wake time anchored.
- **Never compensate with naps longer than 20 minutes.** Long naps reduce sleep pressure and make it harder to fall asleep that night, creating a vicious cycle.

## Pre-Sleep Supplements

These are taken as part of the evening protocol, 30-60 minutes before bed:

| Supplement | Dose | Purpose |
|-----------|------|---------|
| **Magnesium Glycinate** | 400mg | Muscle relaxation, calming effect, supports deep sleep. Most people are deficient. Glycinate form is best absorbed and least likely to cause GI issues. |
| **Zinc Glycinate** | 30mg | Supports recovery, hormone production, immune function during sleep. |
| **Ashwagandha KSM-66** | 1000mg | Lowers cortisol (the stress hormone that keeps you alert). Improves sleep quality and reduces time to fall asleep. |

These aren't sleeping pills — they support the body's natural sleep processes. The effect is cumulative over weeks, not immediate.

## Tracking

### What to Track

Weigh the weekly trends, not individual nights. One bad night doesn't matter. Three bad weeks in a row is a signal to investigate.

| Metric | Target | What It Tells You |
|--------|--------|-------------------|
| **Time to fall asleep** | < 15 min | If consistently > 20 min, your wind-down isn't effective or you're going to bed before you're actually sleepy. |
| **Total sleep** | 7-7.5 hrs | Below 7 hrs consistently = accumulating sleep debt. Above 8.5 hrs consistently = possible underlying issue. |
| **Deep sleep %** | 15-20% | Where physical recovery happens. Low deep sleep often points to alcohol, late caffeine, or elevated core temperature. |
| **REM %** | 20-25% | Where memory consolidation and emotional processing happen. Low REM often points to alarm interruption (waking mid-cycle) or alcohol. |
| **HRV trend** | Increasing over time | Heart rate variability is the best single proxy for recovery. Trending up = adapting well. Trending down = overtraining, stress, or poor sleep. |

### How to Track

A wearable (smart ring, fitness tracker, or smart mattress) gives you the data. Don't obsess over daily numbers — review the weekly averages every Sunday during your weekly review. Look for trends, not data points.

If you don't want to use a wearable, the simplest metric is subjective: **Do you wake up before your alarm feeling rested?** If yes, your sleep is probably fine. If you're dragging yourself out of bed every morning, something needs to change.

## Troubleshooting

When sleep quality declines, run through this checklist before changing anything drastic:

### Can't Fall Asleep

| Check | Fix |
|-------|-----|
| Caffeine after early afternoon? | Hard cutoff — no caffeine after 1:00 PM. Half-life is 5-6 hours, which means caffeine at 2 PM is still 25% active at midnight. |
| Phone in bed? | Move it outside the bedroom. One "quick check" becomes 45 minutes. |
| Skipping wind-down? | Even the compressed 30-min version makes a difference. Going from high stimulation to bed with nothing in between is the most common cause. |
| Room too warm? | Drop to 65-68°F. If you don't have AC, use a fan pointed at the bed. |
| Going to bed before you're sleepy? | Only go to bed when you feel genuinely drowsy — not just tired. If you've been lying awake for 20 minutes, get up, go to another room, read something boring, and return when drowsy. |

### Waking Up at Night

| Check | Fix |
|-------|-----|
| Alcohol? | Even 1-2 drinks fragments sleep architecture. The sleep you get after alcohol is lower quality even if the duration looks fine. |
| Noise variability? | Add white noise or use earplugs. |
| Light leaking in? | Blackout curtains or sleep mask. |
| Bathroom trips? | Stop drinking fluids 2 hours before bed. |
| Stress/racing thoughts? | This is what the journal is for. If it's happening consistently, your evening brain dump isn't thorough enough — spend more time on it. |

### Still Tired After "Enough" Sleep

| Check | Fix |
|-------|-----|
| Sleep schedule inconsistent? | Fix the wake time first. Everything else follows. |
| Low deep sleep? | Check temperature (room too warm), alcohol, and late caffeine. |
| Waking up mid-cycle? | Try shifting bedtime by 15-30 min in either direction. Sleep cycles are ~90 min — waking mid-cycle feels worse than waking at the end of one. |
| Overtraining? | Check HRV trends. If declining despite "enough" sleep, you may need a deload week. |

## The Caffeine Rule

No caffeine after early afternoon — 1:00 PM at the latest. This is the single most impactful dietary change for sleep quality.

Caffeine has a half-life of 5-6 hours. A coffee at 2:00 PM means 50% of that caffeine is still in your system at 8:00 PM and 25% at 2:00 AM. You might fall asleep fine (you're just overriding the alertness with fatigue), but your deep sleep will be significantly reduced.

If you need an afternoon energy boost, try: a 15-20 minute power nap, a short walk outside, cold water on your face, or L-Theanine (which provides calm focus without interfering with sleep).
